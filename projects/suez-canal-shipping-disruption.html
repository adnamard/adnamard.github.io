Can One Ship Break Global Trade?

In March 2021, one grounded ship in the Suez Canal held up roughly 12% of global trade for six days. This project tests whether that kind of shock — or something far more mundane — is really what drives shipping delays.

Python Pandas Plotly

The challenge

Business context

Companies plan supply chains around the assumption that shipping is predictable. Leadership needed to know what actually drives delay risk — vessel age, terminal handling, or one-off shocks like Suez — before deciding where to invest: newer ships, better terminal contracts, or contingency buffers.

Questions I asked

🚢

Are older vessels actually slower?

📍

Which terminals create the real bottlenecks?

🌍

Did the Suez disruption really change operations?

01 / EXPLORE THE DATA

Does an older fleet mean a slower fleet?

I grouped vessels into five age brackets and compared their average transit speed, expecting a clear downward slope as ships got older.

💡 Cargo vessels overall ran slower than expected, but vessel age barely moved the needle — a 20-year-old ship kept nearly the same pace as a 5-year-old one.

02 / CONTINUE THE INVESTIGATION

Which terminals create the real bottlenecks?

Next I compared average dwell time — hours spent waiting or docked — across six major terminals along the route.

💡 One terminal held vessels nearly twice as long as the others — the gap between terminals was far larger than any regional difference in the data.

03 / CONTINUE THE INVESTIGATION

Did the Suez disruption really change operations?

Finally, I plotted daily transit volume through the canal across the six weeks around the March 2021 blockage.

💡 Volume dropped sharply for about a week, then bounced back to normal within a month — a sharp shock, but a short-lived one.

The conclusion, not the beginning

Bringing it together

After validating each hypothesis individually, I summarized everything into one executive dashboard — so a reader can get the whole story in ten seconds if they need to.

Key takeaways

What the data actually said

🚢

The Suez disruption caused only temporary delays — the system recovered within weeks.

📍

Terminal-level differences were larger than regional differences. Where you dock matters more than where you sail.

⚙️

Operational efficiency mattered far more than vessel age — a slower, better-run fleet still wins.

So what should change

Business recommendations

01

Stop budgeting for vessel-age risk. Fleet renewal has almost no effect on transit speed — redirect that capital toward terminal operations instead.

02

Renegotiate or diversify away from the slowest terminal, since it drives more schedule risk than any single global disruption did.

03

Build a lightweight monitoring dashboard on daily transit volume as an early-warning signal for blockage-like anomalies, rather than over-engineering for rare black-swan events.

Reflection

What surprised me most was how little vessel age mattered — I went in assuming it would be the headline finding. I learned that the "obvious" hypothesis is usually the first one worth throwing out. If I did this again, I'd pull in weather and customs-clearance data too, since dwell time is probably not just a terminal problem.
