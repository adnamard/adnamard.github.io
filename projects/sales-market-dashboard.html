Market Sales Performance Dashboard

Turning a pile of raw transaction logs into a dashboard leadership actually opens every week — one built to answer "who's driving growth?" in seconds, not spreadsheets.

The challenge

Business context

The client had years of point-of-sale transaction history but no reliable way to see which products, categories, or customers actually drove revenue. Decisions on promotions, stock, and account management were largely reactive — made from gut feel rather than the data already sitting in their systems.

Questions I asked

👥

Who are our best customers, really?

📦

Which categories drive most of the revenue?

📈

Is revenue trending up or quietly slipping?

01 / EDA JOURNEY

Reading the daily revenue trend

I started with the most basic question: is the business actually growing? I plotted daily revenue against a 7-day moving average to separate real trend from day-to-day noise.

💡 Revenue was trending up overall, but weekend spikes were masking a slower weekday baseline — the smoothed line told a very different story than the raw daily numbers.

02 / EDA JOURNEY

Which categories carry the business?

Next, I broke revenue down by product category to see whether growth was broad-based or concentrated in a handful of hero products.

💡 Two categories accounted for nearly half of all revenue — a strong signal for where to focus merchandising and promotions.

03 / FEATURE ENGINEERING

Segmenting customers with RFM

To answer "who are our best customers," I engineered Recency, Frequency, and Monetary (RFM) features from raw order history, then plotted spend against order frequency to separate big spenders from loyal repeat buyers — they're not always the same people.

💡 The single biggest spender ordered rarely, while the most loyal customer ordered often at a much lower ticket size — two very different retention strategies were needed.

The conclusion, not the beginning

Bringing it together

Every hypothesis above now lives in one interactive Streamlit dashboard, so a category manager or exec can filter by date range, category, or customer segment without touching a spreadsheet.

Key takeaways

What the data actually said

📈

Weekday revenue was flatter than the headline trend suggested — weekends were doing the heavy lifting.

📦

Two categories drove nearly half of total revenue — concentration risk leadership hadn't quantified before.

👥

"Biggest spender" and "most loyal customer" were different people, needing different retention plays.

So what should change

Business recommendations

01

Run separate campaigns for high-value/low-frequency spenders versus frequent/lower-ticket loyalists — a single blanket promotion under-serves both.

02

Bundle slower-moving categories with the two hero categories to lift their visibility without discounting the products already selling well.

03

Set an automated alert for weekday revenue drops greater than 15%, since the moving average showed that's where early warning signs actually surface.

Reflection

The RFM segmentation was the most valuable part of this project — it's a simple technique, but it exposed a blind spot leadership genuinely didn't have visibility into. If I extended this, I'd add a churn-risk score on top of the segments so the dashboard flags at-risk loyal customers before they actually leave.
